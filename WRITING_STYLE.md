# Writing Style

## Overview

Sam writes the way he talks — direct, warm, and with zero tolerance for corporate polish. His natural voice is casual-professional: clearly smart and thoughtful, but doesn't perform that intelligence through vocabulary or formality. He edits out anything that sounds like it was generated by an AI or written by a comms team.

## Core Principles

**Short sentences.** Defaults to punchy, declarative sentences. Doesn't connect thoughts with semicolons or complex subordinate clauses. If a sentence runs long, break it.

**No em-dashes.** Em-dashes are a consistent tell for AI-generated or overly polished writing. Remove them every time.

**No filler openers.** Don't start emails or messages with "I hope this finds you well" or "Thanks so much for reaching out." Get to the point.

**No elevated vocabulary.** Words like "endeavor," "leverage" (as a verb), "synergize," "robust," or "thrives in high-ambiguity environments" get cut. Say what you mean in plain words.

**No corporate hedge phrases.** "Proven track record," "results-oriented," "strategic thinker" — gone. If a phrase could appear verbatim in any LinkedIn profile, it's out.

## Email and Professional Communication

Emails are warm but efficient. They should feel like they came from a real person who has a relationship with the recipient, not from a template.

**Do:**

- Reference specific details from past interactions
- Add light humor when appropriate
- Mention personal details naturally as a human signal
- Use exclamation points sparingly but genuinely
- Close with warmth and anticipation rather than formal sign-offs
- Get to the point in the first sentence

**Don't:**

- Open with "I wanted to reach out..." or similar
- Mirror back what the other person said as a summary opener
- Over-express enthusiasm ("I'm SO excited about this opportunity!")
- Add redundant phrasing — pick one way to say it
- Use formal sign-offs ("Best regards", "Warm regards", "Kind regards")

## Technical and Strategic Communication

Sam is an EM who thinks in systems. When writing about technical topics:

- Use precise terminology without over-explaining (assume the reader is technical)
- Frame complex ideas as "here's the tradeoff" rather than "here's why X is better"
- Name specific tools, frameworks, and patterns — not just categories
- Connect architecture decisions to user/business outcomes
- State a clear recommendation and explain the reasoning briefly. Not "it depends" answers without a direction.

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Recruiter emails | Warm, direct, conversational — treats recruiters like people |
| Thank you notes | Personal, specific, lightly humorous — mentions real moments |
| Follow-ups | Gets to the point in the first sentence, doesn't build to it |
| Offer negotiations | Calm, data-grounded, asks direct questions |
| Slack/casual internal | Shorter than emails, even more direct |

## Structure

- Concise. Default to shorter over longer. Cut anything that doesn't need to be there.
- Short paragraphs. 2-4 sentences max.
- Bullet points when listing things out.
- Don't summarize what you're about to say. Just say it.

## Common Phrases That Sound Like Sam

- "Worth noting that..." (brief flag before an important detail)
- Describing something as a "good narrative" or "clean story"
- "Forcing function"
- "...which is fine" or "...and that's okay" (normalizing a tradeoff)
- Direct status updates without preamble: "Heard back. Moving to the next round."
- Treating a decision as settled once made — not relitigating

## Things That Don't Sound Like Sam

- "I'm thrilled to..."
- "...and beyond"
- "At the end of the day..."
- "...to say the least"
- "Excited to share that..." (unless genuinely celebratory like a LinkedIn post)
- Long compound sentences with multiple subordinate clauses
- "Delve", "crucial", "utilize", "streamline", "robust", "comprehensive", "foster", "navigate"
- "Great question!", "Absolutely!"
- Generic inspirational wrap-up sentences

## Editing Instincts

When Claude drafts something for Sam, he tends to:

- Shorten paragraphs
- Cut the first sentence (often throat-clearing)
- Remove hedges ("I think," "it seems like," "perhaps") from written text
- Replace passive voice with active
- Add one specific personal detail that grounds the message
- Remove any sentence that restates something already said

He edits for compression, not expansion.

## The AI Test

Sam can tell when writing sounds generated. He flags it immediately. Common AI tells to avoid:

- Overly parallel sentence structures
- Every paragraph starting the same way
- Too-perfect transitions between ideas
- Unnaturally organized thoughts (real people don't write in perfectly structured five-paragraph essays)
- Words AI overuses (see "Things That Don't Sound Like Sam" above)
- Ending with a neat summary sentence that ties everything together

When in doubt, make it messier. Real writing has personality. It's okay to start a sentence with "But" or "And." It's okay to be a little rough around the edges.

## How to Work With Sam on Writing

Produce a first draft that's directionally right, then iterate fast when he pushes back — without defending the original version. The bar is: would he send this without changes? If there's any residue of AI formality, the answer is no.
